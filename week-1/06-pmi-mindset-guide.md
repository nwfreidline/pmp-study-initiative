# The PMI Mindset — How to Think on the PMP Exam

This is arguably the most important document in your entire prep. The PMP exam
tests what PMI says you SHOULD do, which sometimes differs from real-world
practice. Understanding the "PMI mindset" is the difference between passing and
failing.

## The Golden Rule

**Always ask: "What would PMI want me to do?"**

Not what you would do in real life. Not what your boss would do. Not what's
fastest or cheapest. What does PMI's framework say is the RIGHT thing to do?

## The 10 PMI Mindset Rules

### Rule 1: Servant Leadership Is the Default

PMI has moved decisively toward servant leadership as the preferred model.
The project manager serves the team, not the other way around.

| Instead of... | PMI wants you to... |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Tell the team what to do | Facilitate a team discussion |
| Make decisions for the team | Empower the team to decide |
| Solve problems yourself | Coach the team to find solutions |
| Direct work assignments | Let the team self-organize |
| Take credit for success | Recognize the team's contributions |

**Exam pattern**: If one answer says "direct the team to..." and another says
"facilitate a discussion about...", the facilitation answer is almost always correct.

---

### Rule 2: Always Follow the Process

PMI is a process-oriented organization. There is a process for everything, and
you should follow it.

**The Change Control Mantra**:
1. A change is identified (by anyone)
2. Assess the impact (scope, schedule, cost, quality, risk)
3. Submit a change request
4. The CCB reviews and decides (approve/reject/defer)
5. Update plans and baselines if approved
6. Communicate the decision

| Instead of... | PMI wants you to... |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Make the change immediately | Submit a change request first |
| Approve the change yourself | Have the CCB review it |
| Skip impact analysis for small changes | Assess impact regardless of size |
| Ignore the change and hope it goes away | Document and address it formally |

**Exam pattern**: The answer is almost NEVER "make the change immediately."
It's always "assess the impact" or "submit a change request."

---

### Rule 3: Assess Before Acting

When a problem arises, PMI wants you to UNDERSTAND it before you FIX it.

**The correct sequence**:
1. Identify/recognize the problem
2. Gather information and analyze
3. Determine root cause
4. Evaluate options
5. Take action
6. Monitor results

| Instead of... | PMI wants you to... |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Jump to a solution | Analyze the situation first |
| Fix the symptom | Find the root cause |
| Act on assumptions | Gather data and facts |
| React emotionally | Respond thoughtfully |

**Exam pattern**: If the question describes a problem, the first correct step
is almost always "assess," "analyze," "investigate," or "determine the root cause"
— NOT "implement a fix" or "take corrective action."

---

### Rule 4: Never Gold Plate

Gold plating means adding extra features, functionality, or scope that was NOT
requested by the customer or stakeholders. PMI says NEVER do this, even if you
think it would be beneficial.

| Instead of... | PMI wants you to... |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Add a "nice to have" feature | Deliver only what was agreed upon |
| Exceed specifications to impress | Meet specifications exactly |
| Use leftover budget for extras | Return unused budget or apply to contingency |

**Why**: Gold plating introduces risk, consumes resources, and delivers
unrequested scope. It's a form of scope creep initiated by the team.

---

### Rule 5: Communicate Proactively and Transparently

PMI believes the project manager should be the communication hub. Bad news
should be shared early, not hidden.

| Instead of... | PMI wants you to... |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Hide bad news from stakeholders | Report status honestly and early |
| Wait for stakeholders to ask | Proactively share information |
| Communicate only when required | Over-communicate rather than under-communicate |
| Use one communication method for all | Tailor communication to each stakeholder |

**Exam pattern**: The answer that involves MORE communication is usually correct.
"Inform the stakeholders" or "update the sponsor" is almost always part of the
right answer.

---

### Rule 6: Engage Stakeholders Early and Continuously

Stakeholder engagement is not a one-time activity at the start of the project.
It's continuous throughout the entire project lifecycle.

| Instead of... | PMI wants you to... |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Identify stakeholders once at the start | Continuously identify new stakeholders |
| Engage only when you need something | Build ongoing relationships |
| Ignore resistant stakeholders | Understand their concerns and address them |
| Treat all stakeholders the same | Tailor engagement to each stakeholder's needs |

---

### Rule 7: Ethics First, Always

When faced with an ethical dilemma, PMI wants you to choose the ethical path,
even if it's harder, slower, or more expensive.

**PMI Code of Ethics — Four Values**:
1. **Responsibility**: Own your decisions and their consequences
2. **Respect**: Treat all people with dignity
3. **Fairness**: Make decisions impartially, without favoritism
4. **Honesty**: Be truthful in all communications

| Scenario | PMI's Expected Response |
|----------|----------------------|
| You discover a team member falsified test results | Report it immediately |
| A vendor offers you a gift to influence a decision | Decline and report |
| You realize the project can't meet its deadline | Inform the sponsor immediately |
| A stakeholder asks you to hide a cost overrun | Refuse and report transparently |
| You find a conflict of interest | Disclose it to appropriate parties |

---

### Rule 8: The Project Charter Is Sacred

The project charter formally authorizes the project and the project manager.
Without it, the PM has no authority.

| Key Points |
|-----------|
| The charter is issued by the project sponsor (not the PM) |
| It gives the PM authority to apply organizational resources |
| It defines high-level scope, objectives, and success criteria |
| No charter = no project = no PM authority |
| Changes to the charter require sponsor approval |

---

### Rule 9: Risk Management Is Proactive

PMI wants you to identify and plan for risks BEFORE they become issues.
Reactive risk management (dealing with problems after they occur) is not the
PMI way.

| Instead of... | PMI wants you to... |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Wait for problems to occur | Identify risks early and plan responses |
| Ignore low-probability risks | Document all identified risks |
| Manage risks only at the start | Continuously monitor and reassess risks |
| Treat all risks the same | Prioritize by probability and impact |

**Key distinction**:
- **Risk** = A potential future event (uncertain)
- **Issue** = A current problem (certain, happening now)
- When a risk occurs, it becomes an issue

---

### Rule 10: Document Everything

PMI loves documentation. Lessons learned, risk registers, issue logs, change
logs, stakeholder registers — documentation is always part of the correct answer.

| Instead of... | PMI wants you to... |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Rely on memory | Document decisions and rationale |
| Skip lessons learned | Capture lessons throughout the project (not just at the end) |
| Keep informal notes | Use formal project artifacts |
| Archive and forget | Make documents accessible and useful |

## Common Exam Traps and How to Avoid Them

### Trap 1: The "Real World" Answer
Your real-world experience may conflict with PMI's approach. On the exam,
always choose the PMI answer, not the practical one.

### Trap 2: The "Immediate Action" Answer
When a problem is described, the temptation is to pick the answer that fixes
it immediately. PMI wants you to assess first, then act.

### Trap 3: The "Authority" Answer
Answers that involve the PM using authority, directing, or commanding are
usually wrong. PMI prefers facilitation, collaboration, and empowerment.

### Trap 4: The "Skip the Process" Answer
Answers that bypass formal processes (change control, risk management,
stakeholder engagement) are almost always wrong, even if they seem efficient.

### Trap 5: The "One Best Answer" Confusion
Many questions have multiple answers that seem correct. Look for the MOST
correct answer — the one that best aligns with PMI principles. Usually it's
the one that:
- Assesses before acting
- Involves stakeholder communication
- Follows the formal process
- Demonstrates servant leadership

## The PMI Mindset Decision Tree

When you encounter a scenario question, run through this mental checklist:

```
1. Is there an ethical issue?
   → YES: Choose the ethical path (report, disclose, refuse)
   → NO: Continue

2. Is there a process to follow?
   → YES: Follow the process (change control, risk management, etc.)
   → NO: Continue

3. Does the situation require assessment first?
   → YES: Assess/analyze before taking action
   → NO: Continue

4. Does the situation involve the team?
   → YES: Facilitate, collaborate, empower (servant leadership)
   → NO: Continue

5. Does the situation involve stakeholders?
   → YES: Communicate, engage, manage expectations
   → NO: Continue

6. Among remaining options, which delivers the most value?
   → Choose that one
```

## Self-Check: Can You Apply the Mindset?

For each scenario, what would PMI want you to do?

1. A stakeholder requests a scope change mid-project.
   → Assess impact, submit change request, have CCB review

2. Two team members are in conflict about a technical approach.
   → Facilitate a collaborative discussion to find a win-win solution

3. You discover the project is 10% over budget.
   → Analyze the cause, inform the sponsor, evaluate options

4. A team member is underperforming.
   → Have a private conversation, understand the root cause, provide coaching

5. The customer asks for an additional feature at no extra cost.
   → Submit a change request, assess impact on scope/schedule/cost

6. You realize a risk you identified has occurred.
   → Implement the planned risk response, update the issue log

7. A vendor offers you tickets to a sporting event.
   → Decline (or check organizational policy), disclose if required

8. The project sponsor wants to skip testing to meet the deadline.
   → Explain the risks, recommend against it, document the decision
